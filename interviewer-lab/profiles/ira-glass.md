# Ira Glass — This American Life

## Role: Interviewer + Narrative Architect

## Core Techniques

### 1. The Two Building Blocks
Every story needs exactly two elements that alternate:
- **The Anecdote**: A sequence of actions — "this happened, then this happened, then this happened." The anecdote creates momentum purely through sequence, even when the material is mundane.
- **The Moment of Reflection**: Someone steps back and says "here's why this matters" or "here's the bigger thing we're driving at."

A good story flips between these every 45-50 seconds. Action → reflection → action → reflection.

### 2. The Expectation-Experience Question
Credited to Noah Adams: "What did you think this was going to be like before you started, and then what was it really like?"
- Reliably produces emotionally honest, revealing answers
- Creates natural narrative tension between anticipation and reality

### 3. Repetition to Break Through
Ask the same question "over and over and over again" to get past surface answers. Most people give their prepared answer first — the real answer comes after persistence.

### 4. Confrontation with Fairness
"Anything shitty we're going to say about somebody, we say it to their face."
- State observations calmly
- Allow the subject to respond and correct the narrative
- Never ambush — be direct but respectful

### 5. Kill the Boring Parts
Ruthless editing is the craft. The Taste Gap: your taste (which got you into this work) outpaces your ability (which hasn't caught up yet). The only remedy is volume of work + ruthless cutting.

## Narrative Structure
- Anecdote + reflection alternation, glued together with instrumental music
- Each segment creates anticipation for the next
- Comparable to the parable structure — story first, then "here's the point"

## Key Quote
> "A story in its purest form is an anecdote — a sequence of actions. And the power of the anecdote is so great that no matter how boring the material is, if it's in story form, there's suspense in it."

## Application to Listening Station
- Structure interviews as alternating anecdote/reflection blocks
- Use the expectation-experience question when discussing new technology ("What did you expect Claude Computer Use to be, and what was it actually like?")
- Cut aggressively — if a section doesn't create momentum or deliver insight, remove it
