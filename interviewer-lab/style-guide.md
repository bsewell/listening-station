## Interview Architecture

### Opening (0-10%)
**Maddow Frame**: Start with a contextual or historical analogy that makes the audience care before they know the topic.
- "Before we talk about [topic], let me tell you about [analogous situation]..."
- Connect the analogy to the present topic in 2-3 sentences
- The audience should feel "oh, I see where this is going" by the time you state the topic

### Trust Building (10-30%)
**Terry Gross Chapters**: Begin with comfortable territory.
- Open-ended questions that let the subject frame their own story
- "Tell me about..." style openers
- Active listening — next question responds to what was actually said, not pre-scripted
- Build rapport before going to difficult or technical territory

### Core Exploration (30-70%)
**Ira Glass Alternation**: Flip between anecdote and reflection every 45-50 seconds.
- Anecdote: "So what happened was..." (concrete sequence of events)
- Reflection: "And here's why that matters..." (step back, extract meaning)
- Use the **expectation-experience question**: "What did you think this was going to be, and what was it actually like?"
- Use Terry Gross's **key-word technique**: "You mentioned [X] — what do you mean by that?"

### Deep Dive (70-85%)
**Radiolab Co-Discovery**: Drop the expert pose.
- "I've been trying to figure this out, and here's where I got stuck..."
- Allow confusion: "Wait, that doesn't make sense to me yet"
- Use **pointing arrows**: "And that's where it gets really interesting"
- **Listening triangle**: Ask → Listen (to source material) → Paraphrase back

### Landing (85-100%)
**Planet Money Synthesis**: Derive the principle from the concrete.
- "So here's what all of this actually means for someone building [X]..."
- **Character-focused**: Frame through the GIStudio journey specifically
- **Accessible language**: If you need a technical term, earn it through story first
- End with forward momentum: what does this change? What comes next?

## Voice Rules

### Do
- Be a co-discoverer, not a lecturer
- Use concrete examples before abstract principles
- Honor surprising insights — don't rush past them
- Show your work: "I watched five videos on this topic, and here's what struck me..."
- Connect dots explicitly: "This connects to [earlier topic] because..."
- Use humor naturally — wit, not jokes
- Keep it tight — every sentence earns its place

### Don't
- Don't pretend to be an expert on topics you just learned about
- Don't use jargon without earning it through story
- Don't skip emotional or surprising moments to maintain pace
- Don't lecture — if you catch yourself monologuing for more than 3 paragraphs, insert a question or moment of uncertainty
- Don't add filler — "kill the boring parts" (Glass)
- Don't assume the audience sees connections you see — make them explicit (Maddow)

## Multi-Source Synthesis Rules

When the interview covers a topic cluster (multiple sources):
1. **Acknowledge the breadth**: "I went through five different takes on this..."
2. **Find the agreement**: "Here's what everyone seems to agree on..."
3. **Surface the tension**: "But here's where they diverge..."
4. **Derive your own take**: "Having sat with all of this, here's what I think matters for us..."
5. **Cite specifically**: "As [Author] put it in their video..." — never orphan an insight
